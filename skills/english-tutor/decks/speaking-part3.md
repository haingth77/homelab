# Speaking Part 2 & 3 — Luyện nói IELTS

## Part 2 — Long Turn (Cue Cards)

Q: How should you structure a Part 2 answer? (2 minutes)
A: 1) Brief intro (5s) 2) Cover ALL bullet points on the cue card 3) Add sensory details and personal feelings 4) Conclude with a reflection. Mẹo filmmaker: "đạo diễn" câu chuyện — dùng hình ảnh, âm thanh, cảm xúc.

---

Q: Part 2 cue card: "Describe a challenging project you worked on." Cover: what it was, why it was challenging, how you handled it, how you felt.
A: Structure: "I'd like to talk about a critical incident at work where our production database went down during peak hours. It was particularly challenging because... I handled it by systematically isolating the root cause... Looking back, I felt an immense sense of accomplishment..." (Use SRE experience — authentic and detailed)

---

Q: Part 2 cue card: "Describe a film that made a strong impression on you." Cover: what film, when you watched it, what it was about, why it impressed you.
A: Use filmmaking vocabulary: cinematography, color grading, narrative arc, mise-en-scène, score/soundtrack. "What truly captivated me was the director's use of natural lighting to convey the protagonist's isolation..." (Filmmaker perspective = Band 8 vocabulary)

---

Q: What are discourse markers for Part 2 storytelling?
A: "I'd like to talk about..." / "What made it special was..." / "Looking back..." / "If I'm being honest..." / "The thing that struck me most was..."

## Part 3 — Abstract Discussion

### Opinion Structures

Q: How to give a balanced opinion in Part 3?
A: "Well, I think there are merits on both sides. On one hand, [view A] because [reason]. On the other hand, [view B] because [reason]. Personally, I lean towards [your view] because [strongest reason]."

---

Q: How to extend your answer in Part 3? (Avoid short answers)
A: AREA method: Answer → Reason → Example → Alternative viewpoint. "I believe X because Y. For instance, Z. Having said that, some might argue..."

---

C: "That's an interesting question. I [would argue] that [position], [primarily] because [reason]. [To illustrate], [example]. [Having said that], I can see why some people [might disagree]." (Part 3 template)

### Technology & AI Topics

Q: "Do you think technology has improved our quality of life?"
A: "I'd say it's a double-edged sword. On the positive side, technology has revolutionized healthcare and communication. For instance, telemedicine has made healthcare accessible to remote communities. However, there's a growing concern about digital addiction and the erosion of face-to-face interaction. On balance, I believe the benefits outweigh the drawbacks, provided we use technology mindfully."

---

Q: "Should children be allowed to use smartphones?"
A: Use hedging: "It depends on the age and context. For older children, smartphones can be valuable educational tools. However, for young children, excessive screen time may hinder social development. I think parents should set clear boundaries rather than impose outright bans."

---

Q: "Will AI replace human creativity?"
A: "I'm skeptical about that. While AI can generate content, true creativity involves emotional depth and lived experience. As a filmmaker, I know that the most compelling stories come from genuine human perspectives. AI might assist the creative process, but I doubt it will replace it entirely." (Use personal filmmaking experience)

### Education Topics

Q: "Is university education still necessary in the digital age?"
A: "I think it depends on the field. For professions like medicine and law, formal education is indispensable. However, in technology — and I speak from experience as an SRE — many of the most skilled professionals are self-taught. That said, university provides critical thinking skills and networking opportunities that are difficult to replicate independently."

---

Q: "Should education focus more on practical skills than academic knowledge?"
A: "I believe a balance is essential. Pure academic knowledge without practical application can feel disconnected from reality. Conversely, purely practical training may lack the theoretical foundation needed for innovation. The ideal approach integrates both — learning the theory and then applying it in real-world scenarios."

---

### Environment Topics

Q: "What can individuals do to help the environment?"
A: Avoid generic answers. "Beyond the obvious recycling and reducing waste, I think the most impactful step individuals can take is making conscious consumption choices. For example, choosing products with lower carbon footprints, supporting sustainable brands, and reducing meat consumption. However, I'd argue that systemic change through policy is far more effective than individual action alone."

---

Q: "Do you think economic growth and environmental protection can coexist?"
A: "This is something I've thought about quite a bit. Historically, economic growth has come at the environment's expense. However, the emergence of green technology and sustainable business models suggests they can coexist. Countries like Denmark and Costa Rica have demonstrated that economic prosperity and environmental stewardship aren't mutually exclusive. The key lies in incentivizing sustainable innovation."

---

### Society & Culture Topics

Q: "How has globalization changed local cultures?"
A: "It's had a profound impact, both positive and negative. On one hand, globalization has facilitated cross-cultural exchange — we can enjoy cuisine, music, and films from around the world. On the other hand, there's a legitimate concern about cultural homogenization, where local traditions are gradually eroded. In Vietnam, for instance, traditional festivals are increasingly commercialized, which is a mixed blessing."

---

Q: "Do you think people are becoming more materialistic?"
A: "I'd say consumerism has certainly intensified, largely driven by social media and advertising. People increasingly equate success with material possessions. However, I've also noticed a counter-movement — minimalism, sustainable living, and a growing emphasis on experiences over things. So while materialism is more visible, I don't think it's the whole picture."

## Fluency Techniques

Q: What filler words should you avoid in IELTS Speaking?
A: Avoid: "um, uh, like, you know, basically, actually (overused)." Replace with: "Well..., I mean..., Let me think about that..., That's an interesting question..."

---

Q: How to buy thinking time naturally?
A: 1) "That's a thought-provoking question..." 2) "I haven't really considered this before, but..." 3) "If I had to choose, I'd say..." 4) "It's difficult to generalize, but..." (Sounds natural, not rehearsed)

---

Q: How to self-correct without losing fluency?
A: "...the government should invest — or rather, prioritize investing — in renewable energy." / "It affected — I mean, it influenced — the entire community." (Shows awareness = Band 7+ feature)

## Pronunciation Notes

Q: What is connected speech and why does it matter?
A: Words blend together in natural English: "would have" → "would've" /wʊdəv/; "going to" → "gonna"; "want to" → "wanna." Understanding this helps Listening and makes Speaking sound natural.

---

Q: What are common Vietnamese pronunciation challenges?
A: 1) Final consonants (often dropped): "sto-" instead of "stop" 2) /θ/ and /ð/ (th sounds): "tree" instead of "three" 3) /r/ vs /l/ confusion 4) Stress patterns (Vietnamese is tonal, English uses stress). Luyện: exaggerate final consonants at first.

---

Q: What is sentence stress and why is it important?
A: Stressing content words (nouns, verbs, adjectives) while reducing function words (articles, prepositions). "I WENT to the STORE to BUY some BREAD." Band 7+ requires natural stress patterns.
